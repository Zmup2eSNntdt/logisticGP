Introduction

This is an introduction to logisticGP package.

Given a response-covariate pair \(\left(y, x^T\right) \in R \times R^p\), Conditional density estimation (CDE) aims to find the density function of \(y\) conditioned on the \(x^T\) slice, denoted by \(f\left(y \mid x^T\right)\). In Bayesian paradigm, we assume prior knowledge on \(f\) and subsequently find posterior estimates of \(f\) given the data.

In [our article], we focus on developing a computationally tractable technique to model the conditional density using Logistic Gaussian Process (LGP) prior. The main idea behind this formulation is to use the triangular basis to approximate the Gaussian process (GP) using a pre-fixed regular grid, as discussed in Maatouk and Bay (2017). The logistic density transform was introduced by Leonard, 1978 and we utilize the foundation to model the conditional density of spatially varying response in the presence of a high dimensional covariate space.

Model

Let us denote \(\mathrm{S}=\{1,2, \ldots, \mathrm{K}\}\) be the set of \(\mathrm{K}\) locations which is assumed to be fixed throughout this article. Call \(\mathrm{y}_{\text {is }} \in(0,1),(\mathrm{i}=1,2, \ldots, \mathrm{n}, \mathrm{s} \in \mathrm{S})\) denote the response (FA measurement) corresponding to \(\mathrm{i}^{\text {th }}\) individual at the \(\mathrm{s}^{\mathrm{th}}\) location and \(\mathrm{X}\) be a \(\mathrm{n} \times \mathrm{p}\) design matrix where the \(\mathrm{i}^{\text {th }}\) row \(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i}}^{\mathrm{T}}=\left(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i} 1}, \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i} 2}, \ldots \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{ip}}\right)\) represent the covariate vector for the \(\mathrm{i}^{\text {th }}\) individual. Furthermore, the rows of \(\mathrm{X}\) are normalized such that \(\max _{\mathrm{i} \in\{1,2, \ldots, \mathrm{n}\}}\left\|\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i}}^{\mathrm{T}}\right\|_2=1\), where \(\|\cdot\|_2\) denote the \(\ell_2\) norm. Let \(\beta(s)\) be the \(\mathrm{p}\)-dimensional vector that varies spatially for each \(\mathrm{s} \in \mathrm{S}\), such that \(\|\beta(\mathrm{s})\|_2=1\). For any fixed \(\mathrm{s} \in \mathrm{S}\), define the following map \(\mathrm{x}_0 \rightarrow \mathrm{w}_{\mathrm{x}_0, \mathrm{~s}}:=\left[\mathrm{x}_0^{\mathrm{T}} \beta(\mathrm{s})+1\right] / 2\). One can see that \(\mathrm{w}_{\mathrm{x}_0, \mathrm{~s}} \in[0,1]\), which easily follows from \(\left|x_o^T \beta(s)\right| \leq\left\|x_o^T\right\|_2 \left\| \beta(s) \right\|_2 \leq 1\). Define \(\Theta:=\left\{(\xi, \tau, \beta): \xi, \tau \in R^{(N+1)^2}, \beta \in R^{{ }^{\mathrm{T}}}\right\}\), given a covariate \(\left(X=x_0^T\right)\) and location ( \(\mathrm{S}=\mathrm{s}\) ), the conditional density of response \((\mathrm{y})\) is modeled using \[ \mathrm{f}_\theta\left(\mathrm{y} \mid \mathrm{X}=\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{o}}^{\mathrm{T}}, \mathrm{S}=\mathrm{s}\right)=\exp \left[\mathrm{g}_1\left(\mathrm{y}, \mathrm{w}_{\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{o}}, \mathrm{s}}\right)+\mathrm{g}_2(\mathrm{y}, \mathrm{s})-\Phi_\theta\left(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{o}}, \mathrm{s}\right)\right] \] where, \(\Phi_\theta\left(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{o}}, \mathrm{s}\right)=\log \int_0^1 \exp \left[\mathrm{g}_1\left(\mathrm{y}, \mathrm{w}_{\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{o}}, \mathrm{s}}\right)+\mathrm{g}_2(\mathrm{y}, \mathrm{s})\right] \mathrm{dy}\) and \(\mathrm{g}_1(\cdot), \mathrm{g}_2(\cdot):[0,1]^2 \rightarrow \mathrm{R}\) are unknown functions, such that \(\mathrm{g}_1\left(\mathrm{z}_1, \mathrm{z}_2\right)=\sum_{\mathrm{ij}=0}^{\mathrm{N}} \xi_{\mathrm{ij}} \mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{i}}\left(\mathrm{z}_1\right) \mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{j}}\left(\mathrm{z}_2\right)\) and \(\mathrm{g}_2\left(\mathrm{z}_1, \mathrm{z}_2\right)=\sum_{\mathrm{ij}=0}^{\mathrm{N}} \tau_{\mathrm{ij}} \mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{i}}\left(\mathrm{z}_1\right) \mathrm{h}_{\mathrm{j}}\left(\mathrm{z}_2\right)\) for any \(\left.\left(\mathrm{z}_1, \mathrm{z}_2\right) \in[0,1]^2\right\}\) and some \(\xi, \tau \in R^{(N+1)^2}\).

Example

We generate the data using 200 observations (n) from each of the 20 locations (K) with a 5 dimensional covariate space (p).

  • Assume \[ \beta_1(s)=s^2+1, \beta_2(s)=(1-s)^2, \beta_3(s)=4 s(1-s), \beta_4(s)=2(s-0.75)^2-1, \beta_5(s)=1.5+4 \sin (s-0.5), \] where \(s\) denote the normalized location on \([0,1]\)
  • \(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i}}^{\mathrm{T}}=\left(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i} 1}, \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i} 2}, \ldots \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{ip}}\right)\) where \(\mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{ij}} \sim \mathrm{U}(-5,5)\) and normalized by \(\max _{\mathrm{i} \in\{1,2, \ldots, \mathrm{n}\}} \| \mathrm{x}_{\mathrm{i}}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{I}_2\)
  • True density \[ \mathrm{f}\left(\mathrm{y}_{\mathrm{is}} \mid \mathrm{x}, \mathrm{s}\right)=\operatorname{Beta}\left[10 \exp \{0.5(\mathrm{~s}-1 / 2)\}, 4(\mathrm{Ks})^{3 / 4}\right] \times 1 /\left[1+\exp \left\{\mathrm{x}^{\mathrm{T}} \beta(\mathrm{s})\right\}\right]+\operatorname{Beta}\left[32 \sin \left(\mathrm{w}_{\mathrm{x}, \mathrm{s}}\right), 8(\mathrm{~s}+1)^{1 / 2}\right] \times 1 /\left[1+\exp \left\{-\mathrm{x}^{\mathrm{T}} \beta(\mathrm{s})\right\}\right] \]

Prior Specification

  • Recall \(\left\{\beta^{\mathrm{T}}(\mathrm{s})=\left[\beta_1(\mathrm{~s}), \beta_2(\mathrm{~s}), \ldots, \beta_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathrm{s})\right]_{1 \times \mathrm{p}}\right\}\) and \(\left\{\beta^{\mathrm{T}}=\left[\beta^{\mathrm{T}}(1), \beta^{\mathrm{T}}(2), \ldots, \beta^{\mathrm{T}}(\mathrm{K})\right]_{1 \times(\mathrm{pK})}\right\}\). Assume that, \(\beta \sim \mathrm{N}_{\mathrm{pK}}(0, \Sigma)\), where \[ \operatorname{Cov}\left(\beta_{1_1}\left(s_1\right), \beta_{1_2}\left(s_2\right)\right)=\sigma_\beta^2 \varrho^{\mathrm{I}\left(1_1 \neq 1_2\right)} \mathrm{e}^{-\phi\left|\mathrm{s}_1-\mathrm{s}_2\right|} . \]

We fixed \(\sigma_\beta^2=1, \varrho=0.10\), and \(\phi=0.10\).

  • We impose Gaussian prior on the parameters \(\xi:=\left\{\xi_{\mathrm{ij}}: 0 \leq \mathrm{i}, \mathrm{j} \leq \mathrm{N}\right\}\) and \(\tau:=\left\{\tau_{\mathrm{ij}}: 0 \leq \mathrm{i}, \mathrm{j} \leq \mathrm{N}\right\}\), such that \[ \operatorname{Cov}\left(\xi_{i_1 j_1}, \xi_{i_2 j_2}\right)=\operatorname{Cov}\left(\tau_{i_1 j_1}, \tau_{i_2 j_2}\right)=\Gamma_{v, \ell}\left(\left(u_{i_1}, u_{j_1}\right),\left(u_{i_2}, u_{j_2}\right)\right) \quad\left(0 \leq i_1, j_1, i_2, j_2 \leq N\right)=: \Gamma_{v, \ell}^N . \] where, \(\Gamma_{v, \ell}(\cdot, \cdot)\) be the Matern covariance with smoothness \(v\) and length-scale \(\ell\). We fixed the smoothness parameter \(v=2.5\), which makes the GP prior smooth enough and \(\ell=0.292\) is chosen so that the correlation at a maximum possible separation \(\Gamma_{v, \ell}((0,0),(1,1)) \approx 0.005\).

p1=5 ## number of co-variates

n2 = 0.2 * n1 ## test set
RNGkind(sample.kind = "Rejection")
set.seed(403) ## setting seed
index.sample = sample(1:n1,size=n2,replace = F) ## random indices for test set
## define beta0(s)
beta0_func<- function(j,x){
  if(j==1)
    return(x^2+1) ## beta0_1(x)
  if(j==2)
    return((1-x)^2) ## beta0_2(x)
  if(j==3)
    return(4*x*(1-x)) ## beta0_3(x)
  if(j==4)
    return(-1 + 2*(x-0.75)^2) ## beta0_4(x)
  if(j==5)
    return(1.5 + 4*sin(x-0.5)) ## beta0_5(x)
  else
    return("wrong input")
}

beta0.true<-matrix(0,nr=p1,nc=K1) ## generate beta_0.true
for(j in 1:p1){
  beta0.true[j,]<- (matrix(beta0_func(j, seq(0,1,length.out = K1)),nr=1))
}

X1<- matrix(runif(n1*p1,-5,5),nc=p1) ## generate covariates

## generate and normalize covariates
X1<- apply(X1,MARGIN = 2,FUN = function(x)(x-mean(x)))
max_norm<- max(sqrt(rowSums(X1*X1)))
X2<- X1/max_norm

## normalize beta_0.true
beta<-beta0.true
beta0.true<- t(t(beta)/sqrt(colSums(beta * beta)))

## generate y
W_0 <-(kronecker(diag(ncol(beta)),X2) %*% matrix(as.vector(beta0.true),nc=1) + 1)/2
W_1 <- 2*(W_0-0.5) #Centralize the probability
y_vec<-numeric(nrow(X2) * ncol(beta0.true))
y1<- matrix(0,nr=n1,nc=K1)
for (j in 1:K1) {
  for(i in 1:n1){

    y_vec[(j-1)*nrow(X2) + i]<-ifelse(runif(1)<=1/(1+exp(W_1[(j-1)*nrow(X2) + i])),
                                      rbeta(1,10*exp((j/ncol(beta0.true)-0.5)/2),4*j^{3/4}),
                                      rbeta(1,32*sin(W_0[(j-1)*nrow(X2) + i]),
                                            8*(j/ncol(beta0.true) +1)^{1/2}))

  }
}

Application to Real Data

Introduction

We are interested in estimating the density of Fractional Anisotropy (FA) at a specific location given seven covariates including the gender variable (\(123\) male and \(91\) female, coded by a dummy variable indicating for male), the age of the subject (years, ranges from \(48.4\) (years) to \(90.4\), mean \(73.20\)), an indicator for handiness (\(193\) righthand and \(20\) left-hand, coded by a dummy variable indicating for left-hand), the education level (years, ranges from \(9\) to \(20\) (years), mean \(15.91\)), an indicator for Alzheimers disease (AD) status \((19.7\%)\), an indicator for mild cognitive impairment (MCI) status \((55.1\%)\) and Mini-Mental State Exam (MMSE) of ADNI. We standardized all variables to have mean zero and variance one.

Given a specific location ( \(s\) ) and a covariate ( \(x\) ), we only have one corresponding response (y), hence it is impossible to comprehend the full competence of the model. Marginalizing over the covariate produces conditional density at a specific location. This enables better visualization of the proposed method due to the availability of multiple responses from the same location. The model has been trained on the full dataset. We selected four locations (out of a total of 83 locations) representing the spread of the data to highlight the robustness of the model. We calculated the density at location \(s\) by \[ \hat{f}(y \mid s)=\frac{1}{n_2 L} \sum_{i=1}^{n_2} \sum_{l=1}^L \hat{f}_{\theta^{(1)}}\left(y \mid x^{(i)}, s\right), \] using \(\mathrm{L}\) posterior samples (we took 2500) of \(\theta, \mathrm{n}_2\) is the number of individuals (which is 42 in this case) that have been randomly selected for ease of computation. We overlay the \(y_{\text {test }}\) with the estimated density for each location.
